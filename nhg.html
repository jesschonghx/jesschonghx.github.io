About

As part of Singapore’ s national initiative, Healthier Sg, the Partners’ portal was developed. It aims to connect residents to community care and centralise programme management for NHG’s (National Healthcare Group) community partners. Through the Partners’ portal, community partners can upload their programmes which are accessible by residents via the NHG Cares App.

Gathering Requirements

To understand the requirements, we held meetings with NHG users to explore their structure, needs, and challenges. While NHG users wanted tools to share and visualise information, community partners needed to track attendance, view data, and issue certificates. Another challenge was to make sure that our design would allow for the synchronisation of data across different platforms i.e. our database to NHG’s Population Health CRM.

Using FigJam, we mapped out user flows based on these insights, aligning with stakeholders before starting the design phase.

Getting things off the ground

To ensure that we were on the right path, we explored apps in the fitness industry with Healthy365, ClassPass, and TeamUp identified as potential benchmarks. Due to limited access to Healthy365's vendor application, we focused on ClassPass and TeamUp.

A heuristic evaluation was conducted based on collected screenshots to assess their usability and features. We then applied what we thought would be feasible for our project.

Explicit Listening

In his book “Articulating Design Decisions”, Greever talked about explicit listening, which are explicit activities we use to improve our listening. For meetings, this can come in the form of taking notes, repeating and rephrasing what you have heard.

Though we were already taking minutes, the notes were long and we often find ourselves lost in the sea of information. I wanted to take better notes to get the most out of the meetings, hence I added some tweaks to make sure my notes are:

• Accessible - Notes are shared in the Notion project folder

• Organised - Headings and bullet points

• Definitive - When a decision is made, make it explicitly clear i.e. Final: Remove button

• Actionable - Include a follow-up action section, and the person in charge

• Forward looking - Highlight certain points that may be up for discussion in the next     meeting

This proved to be very useful when we need to revisit our meeting minutes to follow up on work.

Drawing Attention

We began creating high-fidelity wireframes after mapping out user flows and aligning them with stakeholders.

Since we couldn’t directly engage with the community partners—the primary users of the application—we continuously asked ourselves as we design: Who are the users, and what are their tasks and workflows?

We also made sure to create visual hierarchy on the page to help guide the community partners through a page.

UAT and Launch

Once the product was developed, we conducted User Acceptance Testing (UAT) based on a detailed UAT brief outlining various scenarios. The testing group included NHG users and community partners from the Tsao Foundation and AWWA (Asian Women’s Welfare Association).

During the testing, I worked closely with users, guiding them through the test cases to ensure a smooth process. All bugs, issues, and feedback were documented and shared with the team for prioritisation and delegation.

Following multiple UAT sessions and iterative fixes, the application successfully launched in April 2024. It onboarded over 20 community partners, streamlining more than 400 programs across approximately 100 sites in Central and North Singapore. The launch was also highlighted on GovInsider, showcasing its impact on integrating health and social ecosystems (Read more).

Learnings

Working on this project gave me invaluable experience collaborating with external stakeholders—a process that was both challenging and rewarding.

One practice I embraced was leading with a "Yes"—acknowledging stakeholder input positively by saying, “Yes, that’s a good point.” or “Yes, I understand where you are coming from.” This approach fosters collaboration, reinforcing that we’re all on the same team working toward the same goals. It doesn’t imply immediate commitment but conveys that their contributions are valued.

I also learnt the importance of resourcefulness. When user testing wasn’t feasible, I sought alternative ways to design effectively. This included focusing on understanding the goals and priorities of target users and constantly reflecting on how community partners would use the application in real-world scenarios.
